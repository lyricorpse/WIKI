Fourier Analysis (MATH 627)¶

Fourier coefficient and serires¶

The \(n\)-th Fourier coefficient of \(f(x)\) (defined on the interval \([-\pi,\pi]\)) is

\[\hat{f}(n) = a_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x) e^{-inx} dx, n \in \mathbb{Z},\]

and the Fourier series of \(f\) is

\[f(x) = \sum_{-\infty}^{\infty} a_n e^{inx}, x \in [-\pi, \pi].\]

An important identity of Fourier series:

\[\hat{f}'(n) = in\hat{f}(n), \text{ for all }n\in\mathbb{Z}.\]

Partial sum¶

The \(N\)-th partial sum of the Fourier series of \(f\) (defined on the interval \([-\pi,\pi]\)) is a trigonometric polynomial given by

\[\begin{split}S_N(f)(x) = \sum_{n=-N}^{N}\hat{f}(n)e^{inx}, N>0.\end{split}\]

With the Dirichlet kernel and the idea of convolutions, it follows that

\[\begin{split}S_N(f)(x) &= \sum_{n=-N}^{N}\hat{f}(n)e^{inx} \\ &= \sum_{n=-N}^{N} \left(\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(\xi)e^{-in\xi}d\xi\right) e^{inx} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(\xi)\left(\sum_{n=-N}^{N} e^{in(x-\xi)}\right)d\xi \\ &= (f*D_N)(x).\end{split}\]

Convolutions¶

Given two \(2\pi\)-periodic integrable functions \(f\) and \(g\) on \(\mathbb{R}\), we define their convolution \(f*g\) on \([-\pi, \pi]\) by

\[(f*g)(x) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(\xi)g(x-\xi) d\xi.\]

Since functions are periodic, we can change variables to see that

\[(f*g)(x) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x-\xi)g(\xi) d\xi.\]

Convolutions can be interpreted as weighted averages, in this context, the convolution

\[(f*K_n)(x) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x-\xi)K_n(\xi) d\xi\]

is the average of \(f(x-\xi)\), where the weights are given by \(K_n(y)\).

Some propositions (\(f,g,h\) are \(2\pi\)-periodic integrable functions):

\[\begin{split}& f*(g+h) = (f*g) + (f*h), \\ & (cf)*g = c(f*g) - f*(ch) \text{ for any }c\in\mathbb{C}, \\ & f*g=g*f, \\ & (f*g)*h = f*(g*h), \\ & f*g \text{ is continuous}, \\ & \widehat{f*g}(n) = \hat{f}(n)\hat{g}(n).\end{split}\]

Kernels¶

Good kernels¶

Good kernels should satisfie three properties:

  • For all \(n\ge 1\),

    \[\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}K_n(x)dx = 1.\]

  • There exists \(M>0\) such that for all \(n\ge 1\),

    \[\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}|K_n(x)|dx \le M.\]

  • For all \(\delta > 0\),

    \[\int_{\delta\le |x| \le \pi}|K_n(x)|dx \rightarrow 0~(n\rightarrow \infty).\]

Also, the family of good kernels is refereed to as an approximation to the identity because

\[\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty}(f*K_n)(x) = f(x).\]

Dirichlet kernel¶

(1)\[D_N(x) = \sum_{n=-N}^{N}e^{inx}.\]

Also, we have

\[D_N(x) = \frac{\sin[(N+1/2)x]}{\sin(x/2)}.\]

Fejer kernel¶

(2)\[F_N(x) = \frac{D_0(x) + \cdots + D_{N-1}(x)}{N}\]

where \(D_n(x) (n=0,\dots, N-1)\) is the Dirichlet kernel.

Also, we have

\[F_N(x) = \frac{1}{N} \frac{\sin^2(Nx/2)}{\sin^2(x/2)}.\]

Poisson kernel¶

\[\begin{split}P_r(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}r^{|n|}e^{inx}, 0\le r < 1.\end{split}\]

Summability¶

Cesaro summability¶

Define the \(n\)-th partial sum of a series of complex numnbers \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_k\) as \(s_n\), then the \(N\)-th Cesaro mean of the sequence \(\{s_k\}\) or the Cesaro sum of the series \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_k\) is

\[\sigma_N = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}s_k.\]

If \(\sigma_N\) converges to a limit \(\sigma\) as \(N\rightarrow \infty\), we say that the series sum_{k=0}^{infty}c_k is Cesaro summable to \(\sigma\).

The \(N\)-th Cesaro mean of the Fourier series is defined as

\[\sigma_N(f)(x) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}S_{k}(f)(x) = (f*F_N)(x),\]

where \(F_N(x)\) is the \(N\)-th Fejer kernel.

Abel summability¶

A series of complex numbers \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_k\) is said to be Abel summable to \(s\) if for every \(0\le r<1\), the Abel mean

\[A(r) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_k r^k\]

converges, and

\[\lim_{r\rightarrow 1}A(r) = s.\]

The Abel mean of Fourier series is

\[A_r(f)(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}r^{|n|} a_n e^{in x} = (f*P_r)(x),\]

where \(P_r(x)\) is the Poisson kernel.

Inequalities¶

Triangle inequality¶

In a normed vector space \(V\), for all \(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\in V\), we have

\[\Vert \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y} \Vert \le \Vert \mathbf{x} \Vert + \Vert \mathbf{y} \Vert.\]

Cauchy–Schwarz inequaliy¶

For all vectors \(x,y\) of an inner product space it is true that

\[|(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})| \le \Vert \mathbf{x} \Vert \Vert \mathbf{y} \Vert,\]

where \((\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\) is the inner product.

Minkowski inequality¶

Let \(S\) be a measure space, the Minkowski inequality is the triangle inequality in \(L^p(S)\) with \(1\le p\le\infty\).

For all \(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\in S\), we have

\[\Vert \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y} \Vert_p \le \Vert \mathbf{x} \Vert_p + \Vert \mathbf{y} \Vert_p.\]
